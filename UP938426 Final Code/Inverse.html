Inverse Matrices

If An x n is a square matrix and there is A x A-1 = A-1 x A = 1 then A-1 is the inverse of A.

If |A| = 0, A does NOT have an inverse OR if |A| â‰  0, A does have an inverse.

Method
    Transpose Matrix A to get AT.
    Replace each element of AT by its cofactor:
    adj(A) => adjoint matrix
    Divide by the determinant of A: A-1 = adj(A) / |A|
For 2x2 matrices only:
A

\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4\\ 3 & 7\end{bmatrix}

then;

Det (A) = (ad-cb) = (2 * 7) - (3 * 4) = 2

A-1= (1/Det(A)) x \begin{bmatrix} d & -b\\ -c & a\end{bmatrix}
A-1= (1/2) \begin{bmatrix} 7 & -4\\ -3 & 2\end{bmatrix}
A-1= \begin{bmatrix} 7/2 & -2\\ -3/2 & 1\end{bmatrix}
3 x 3 Matrices

To calculate the inverse of a 3x3 Matrix you need to first find the determinant. If the determinant is 0, then your work is finished, because the matrix has no inverse.

To calculate the determinant the following method is used. This can be revised in the determinate lesson.

Matrix A

\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i\end{bmatrix}

Det(A)) = a * \begin{bmatrix} e & f \\ h & i\end{bmatrix} - b * \begin{bmatrix} d & f \\ g & i\end{bmatrix} + c * \begin{bmatrix} d & e \\ g & h\end{bmatrix}

Once this is done the matrix needs to be transposed.

\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a & d & g \\ b & e & h \\ c & f & i\end{bmatrix}

The third step is to find the determinant of each of the 2x2 minor matricies. To find the right minor matrix for each term, first highlight the row and column of the term you begin with. This should include five terms of the matrix. The remaining four terms make up the minor matrix. This can be revised in the determinate lesson.

Example

\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i\end{bmatrix}

a = Det \begin{bmatrix} e & f \\ h & i\end{bmatrix}      f = Det \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ g & h\end{bmatrix}

Once you have replaced all of the values in this way the matrix needs to be turned into a matrix of cofactors by reversing the signs on alternating terms, like a ceckerboard. This will give you the Adjoint Matrix

\begin{bmatrix} + & - & + \\ - & + & - \\ + & - & + \end{bmatrix}

Now for the final step. Take the Adjoint Matrix and divide it by the determinate.

Inverse Matrix = (1/Det(Matrix)) * Adj(Matrix)

Why do we need to know how to do the inverse of matrices?

As you can't divide matrices you need to have a different way to achieve a similar effect. For example we have XA = B, we want to find matrix X and we know A and B. If we multiply both sides by A-1 we get XAA-1 = BA-1. As AA-1 = 1 we are left with X = BA-1, allowing us to then find X.
